# **The Question That Just Got Much More Complicated**

Should you be saving into a pension or an ISA? The answer used to be simple. It no longer is.

18 min read  ·  Updated June 2026

For most of the past twenty years, the pension versus ISA debate had a clear winner. Put money in a pension first. The upfront tax relief — 20%, 40%, or 45% depending on your tax rate — was simply too good to pass up. An ISA was useful, flexible, and worth having. But it was second. Always second.

That conventional wisdom has not been wrong. For people in work, accumulating savings for retirement, it remains broadly correct. But a significant change in the law — already passed, coming into effect in April 2027 — has shifted the ground considerably for anyone who is approaching retirement or already in it.

From April 2027, pension pots will be included in estates for Inheritance Tax purposes. For decades, leaving money in a pension was one of the most IHT-efficient things a wealthy person could do. From next year, that advantage disappears. And for some families, the tax consequences of leaving a large pension pot unspent are severe enough to be called, without much exaggeration, a disaster.

This article covers both sides of the question: how to think about pension versus ISA during your working years, and why the rules of the game have changed so dramatically for those in or near retirement.

***The pension was the most tax-efficient way to pass on wealth. It is about to become one of the least.***

**Part One: While You Are Still Working**

For those in employment, the pension case remains powerful. The upfront tax relief is the starting point. A higher rate taxpayer contributing £1,000 to a pension gives up £600 of take-home pay. An additional rate taxpayer gives up £550. In a salary sacrifice scheme, where the contribution reduces your gross salary before National Insurance is calculated, the effective cost is lower still.

No ISA matches this. An ISA accepts post-tax money. What it offers in return is clean, uncomplicated access — withdrawals are completely tax-free, at any age, with no requirement to annuitise or take drawdown. The pension taxes you on the way out. The ISA does not.

For most working-age people the right answer remains: maximise the pension first, particularly through salary sacrifice if your employer offers it. Then use the ISA for money you might need before retirement, or as a supplement once the pension is well funded.

The one complication for higher earners is the Annual Allowance — £60,000 per year in 2026/27, including employer contributions. Above this, pension contributions trigger a tax charge. For those earning enough to hit this limit, the ISA becomes the natural overflow vehicle.

This part of the answer has not changed. What follows has.

**Part Two: The Change That Changes Everything**

**This is now law.**

Finance Act 2026 received Royal Assent on 18 March 2026. From 6 April 2027, most unused pension funds and lump-sum death benefits will be included in the value of a deceased person’s estate for Inheritance Tax purposes.

This is not a proposal or a consultation. It is enacted legislation.

To understand why this matters, you need to understand how pensions have worked for IHT purposes until now.

Most modern pension schemes operate through a discretionary trust structure. When a member dies, the scheme trustees or administrators exercise their discretion over who receives the death benefits. Because the member has no fixed entitlement to the funds — the trustees decide — the pension pot has not formed part of the member's estate for IHT purposes.

This made pensions extraordinarily efficient for passing wealth. A couple with a house, savings, and a large pension pot could structure their affairs so that the pension passed to children free of IHT, while the other assets absorbed the tax. Many people specifically chose to spend ISAs, savings, and property equity first, preserving the pension pot precisely because of this advantage.

From April 2027, that logic inverts. The pension will be treated like any other asset. It will sit in the estate. It will be taxed at 40% on everything above the nil-rate band thresholds. And then, for beneficiaries who draw from the inherited pension, Income Tax will apply on top.

**The Double Tax Problem**

The most important practical consequence of the change is not the IHT itself. It is what happens when you combine IHT on the pension with Income Tax on withdrawals by the beneficiary.

Consider the worst case. You die after age 75 with a pension pot that falls within your taxable estate. Your estate pays 40% IHT on the pension. Your adult children then inherit what remains and draw from it. As higher rate taxpayers, they pay 40% Income Tax on every withdrawal. As additional rate taxpayers, they pay 45%.

**The arithmetic of the worst case:**

£100,000 pension pot. Estate pays 40% IHT: £40,000.

Beneficiary receives £60,000. Pays 45% Income Tax on withdrawals: £27,000.

**Net received: £33,000 from a £100,000 pot. Effective combined rate: 67%.**

This analysis is consistent with Deloitte’s published commentary on the legislation.

The government has included a provision intended to prevent pure double taxation. Income Tax will not be payable on the portion of the inherited pension equivalent to the IHT already paid. In theory, this means the effective rate should be lower than 67%.

In practice, the relief is administratively complicated. The beneficiary may need to pay Income Tax on withdrawals first and then reclaim the overpaid amount later. This requires coordination between the pension scheme administrator, the estate's executors, and HMRC. The government has given personal representatives the power to issue a withholding notice instructing the pension scheme to hold back up to 50% of the funds for up to fifteen months from the date of death.

Fifteen months is a long time to wait for a substantial sum of money that is theoretically yours.

**What This Looks Like in Practice**

Numbers become real when you attach them to a person. So consider Margaret.

Margaret is 78. She is widowed. She has a defined benefit pension that pays her a comfortable income — enough that she has never needed to touch her defined contribution pension pot, which has grown to £400,000. She owns her home outright, valued at £450,000. She has £100,000 in ISAs and savings.

**Her total estate from April 2027: £950,000.**

The available reliefs are her nil-rate band of £325,000 and her residence nil-rate band of £175,000, giving £500,000 in total. Everything above that is taxable.

**IHT on £450,000 at 40%: £180,000.**

Her adult children inherit the remaining estate. The pension pot passes to them — but it is an inherited pension, and when they draw from it, they will pay Income Tax on the withdrawals, subject to the partial relief mechanism described above.

Under the old rules — the rules that applied until April 2027 — her estate for IHT purposes would have been £550,000 (house plus savings, excluding the pension). After the nil-rate and residence nil-rate bands, taxable estate: £50,000. IHT: £20,000. Her children would have inherited the entire pension pot free of both IHT and Income Tax.

***The same assets. The same family. A tax bill that grew from £20,000 to £180,000 — plus Income Tax on the pension withdrawals.***

Margaret is not unusual. Her situation describes a significant portion of the retired population in the south-east of England, where property values have pushed many ordinary estates well above the IHT threshold. The pension pot that was her family’s most valuable inheritance has become its most heavily taxed asset overnight.

**What to Do About It**

The conventional wisdom for those in retirement has been: spend your ISAs and savings first. Leave the pension untouched. Let it grow, outside your estate, until your family inherits it tax-free.

That strategy made sense under the old rules. Under the new ones, it may be exactly the wrong approach.

The question is not whether to leave a pension or not. It is a question of which assets your family would prefer to inherit — and in what form. Given that ISA money passes free of Income Tax to beneficiaries, while pension money (from April 2027) will face both IHT and potentially Income Tax, the relative attractiveness of the two vehicles has shifted considerably.

Several strategic responses are worth considering. These are not prescriptions — every family’s situation is different, and the right answer depends on the size of the estate, the beneficiaries’ tax positions, and a range of other factors that only a qualified adviser can assess properly. But as a framework for thinking about the problem:

**Draw down the pension strategically during your lifetime.** For someone like Margaret, drawing from the pension each year up to the point where no higher rate tax is triggered — £50,270 in 2026/27, or more if other income is low — and paying 20% Income Tax voluntarily is preferable to leaving the pot for a beneficiary to pay a combined 67% on. The 20% paid now is the cost of avoiding a much larger bill later.

**Top up ISAs with pension withdrawals.** Once pension income is drawn, it can be invested into an ISA. ISA money grows free of Income Tax and Capital Gains Tax. When it passes to a surviving spouse it retains its ISA wrapper. When it eventually forms part of an estate it will be subject to IHT — but not to Income Tax on withdrawal, which removes one layer of the double tax problem.

**Make gifts during your lifetime.** Every individual has an annual gift exemption of £3,000. Gifts to individuals that survive seven years are outside the estate entirely. These are not new tools. But the calculus around using them has shifted: the pension pot that once sat conveniently outside the estate now needs to be reduced by other means.

**The spouse exemption remains intact.** Pension funds passed to a surviving spouse who is a UK resident are still free of IHT. The problem arrives at the second death, when the surviving spouse’s estate — now including the inherited pension — passes to the next generation. Planning needs to look two deaths ahead, not one.

**The ISA: Relatively More Attractive**

The ISA has not changed. It was never outside the estate for IHT purposes — ISA money has always been included in the taxable estate. The ISA’s tax advantages are clean withdrawals and tax-free growth, not IHT efficiency.

What has changed is the relative position. Until now, the pension had a significant IHT advantage over the ISA. From April 2027, that advantage is gone. On a level playing field for IHT, the ISA’s other qualities — tax-free withdrawals, access at any age, no minimum drawdown requirements, no requirement to crystallise at any point — look more attractive than they did before.

For those still accumulating, the pension’s upfront tax relief still makes it the primary vehicle. But for those in decumulation — drawing down assets in retirement — the ISA has moved from a supplement to a genuine strategic alternative.

**The Limits of General Guidance**

This article has described the mechanics of the change and the broad strategic responses available. It has not told you what to do — because it cannot. The right answer depends on your estate value, your pension pot size, your beneficiaries’ tax positions, whether you have a surviving spouse, your own income needs in retirement, and a range of other factors that interact in ways that require proper professional assessment.

The April 2027 change is exactly the kind of structural shift where the gap between people who take advice and people who do not widens significantly. The tax bill on an unplanned estate can now be several times higher than on a planned one, for the same underlying assets.

If your estate — including your pension — is likely to be above the IHT threshold, the cost of a conversation with a qualified financial adviser and an estate planning solicitor is likely to be the best money you spend this year. The deadline is not April 2027. The decisions that affect your April 2027 position need to be made well before then.
