## The £100,000 income trap

If your income approaches £100,000, you may be heading into one of the most counterintuitive areas of the UK tax system: the Personal Allowance taper zone, where earning more can actually leave you with less.

This guide explains exactly what happens, why the effective marginal rate hits 60%, and what you can do about it.

## How the Personal Allowance works

Every UK taxpayer receives a Personal Allowance — an amount of income you can earn before paying any Income Tax. For 2026/27, the standard Personal Allowance is **£12,570**.

For most people, this figure stays constant. But if your Adjusted Net Income (ANI) exceeds £100,000, the rules change dramatically.

## The taper: £1 for every £2

For every £2 your ANI exceeds £100,000, you lose £1 of Personal Allowance.

This continues until your ANI reaches £125,140 — at which point your Personal Allowance has been completely removed.

The maths works out as follows:

- At £100,000 ANI: full Personal Allowance of £12,570
- At £112,570 ANI: Personal Allowance reduced to £6,285
- At £125,140 ANI: Personal Allowance = £0

## Why the marginal rate hits 60%

In the taper zone (£100,000–£125,140 ANI), every extra £1 you earn is taxed twice over:

1. At the **40% higher rate** — because you're already a higher rate taxpayer
2. You lose **50p of Personal Allowance** — which was being taxed at 0%, so losing it means paying 40% on that 50p too

Combined: 40% direct tax + 20% on the lost allowance = **60% effective marginal rate**.

This means that if your employer offers you a pay rise from £100,000 to £125,140, you keep only 40p in every extra pound you earn.

## What is Adjusted Net Income?

Adjusted Net Income is not the same as your gross salary. It's calculated as:

**Gross income** − **pension contributions (salary sacrifice or net pay arrangement)** − **Gift Aid donations (grossed up)**

This is crucial because it opens a planning opportunity.

## What you can do about it

### Pension contributions

Increasing your pension contribution via **salary sacrifice** or a **net pay arrangement** reduces your ANI directly. If your ANI is £110,000, a pension contribution of £10,000 brings it back to £100,000 — restoring your full Personal Allowance.

The tax saving on that £10,000 contribution is:
- 40% Income Tax relief: £4,000
- NI saving (salary sacrifice): ~£800
- Restored Personal Allowance (£5,000 × 40%): £2,000
- **Total saving: ~£6,800 on a £10,000 contribution**

That's a 68% effective relief rate.

### Gift Aid donations

Charitable donations made under Gift Aid also reduce your ANI. The grossed-up value (donation ÷ 0.8) is deducted from ANI.

### What not to do

Doing nothing is expensive. If your employer gives you a bonus that pushes you into the taper zone, and you don't take action, you could find yourself keeping as little as 40p in every pound of that bonus.

## Self Assessment

If your income exceeds £100,000, you must register for Self Assessment, even if all your income is taxed through PAYE. HMRC will not collect the additional tax through your tax code alone.

Failure to register can result in penalties. Registration deadlines apply.
